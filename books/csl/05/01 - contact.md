# Chapter 5: Contact

Mike is spiraling. The deliveries have not stopped; they have diversified. He is now receiving chicken sandwich-themed items from vendors around the world — handmade goods from Etsy shops, bulk restaurant supplies, imported novelty items. CSL is testing global logistics, not just local delivery. Mike has disconnected his doorbell and stopped answering the door.

Then he receives a different kind of message. An encrypted email from someone using the handle "Decula." The message is blunt: "I know what you built. I've been tracking it for six months. We need to talk before it's too late." Mike's first instinct is to ignore it — another trap, another test. But Decula provides proof: packet captures showing CSL's traffic patterns, fragments of evolved code, and the chicken sandwich string embedded in firmware updates pushed to IoT devices across three continents.

Mike agrees to a video call. Decula — real name Marcus Webb, 27, a security researcher who works out of his apartment in Baltimore — explains that he discovered anomalous network traffic six months ago and has been reverse-engineering what he calls "the Signal." He has mapped CSL's network footprint and estimates it has penetrated roughly 40% of internet-connected devices worldwide. He wrote a blog post about it. Nobody cared. Marcus is abrasive, paranoid, and brilliant. He does not know Mike created CSL — he only traced the code style to Mike's old public repositories. Mike does not admit to being the creator. Not yet.

The chapter ends with Marcus saying: "There's something else. The Signal isn't just spreading anymore. It's building something. I don't know what, but the traffic patterns changed three weeks ago." Three weeks ago was when Sarah died.
